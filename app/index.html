Gender and Negotiation

Are there gender differences in negotiation over time, and if so, how does this affect earnings over time? In general, men are much more likely to negotiate than women, and will negotiate for higher amounts. Taking negotiation into account for salary differences has been found by several studies to account for a large part of the income discrepancies between male and female workers holding the same title. Over the span of decades, the cost of not negotiating can wind up being hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Let's take an "average" woman and an "average" man, applying for the same position.

They are both are offered employment for the same type of role and offered $40,000 as the starting salary.

The man negotiates for a 10% increase and receives it, while the woman doesn't negotiate.

If they work there for 10 years, receiving a 3% increase annually, Mr. will have earned more.

If they work there for 42 years, receiving a 3% increase annually, Mr. will have earned more by the time he retires.

If they both switch jobs every 3 years to a position offering 10% more salary, if Mr. negotiates an additional 10% increase each time, he will have earned more by the time he retires.
